# Legal exposure for "What Would the Critics Say?" AI art criticism tool

**The feature carries moderate overall legal risk, with the strongest exposure in right of publicity — not copyright or defamation.** The single most important finding: this tool is almost certainly *not* legal parody under current case law, which undermines the site owner's core defensive intuition. However, the non-commercial/educational nature of the project, combined with strong First Amendment protections for criticism and commentary, significantly reduces practical exposure. With proper disclaimers and modest structural changes, the risk profile drops to low-to-moderate for most claims.

One factual correction: **Dave Hickey died on November 12, 2021**, in Santa Fe, New Mexico. He is not a living critic. This means two of the six named critics are deceased, which matters for the analysis below.

---

## Copyright is essentially a non-issue — style cannot be owned

This is the clearest area of the analysis. **Writing style, analytical methodology, and critical voice are not copyrightable under U.S. law.** The idea/expression dichotomy in 17 U.S.C. § 102(b) explicitly excludes "any idea, procedure, process, system, method of operation, concept, principle, or discovery" from copyright protection. A critic's analytical framework is precisely that — a method, a system, an approach.

The Ninth Circuit stated this directly in *Tangle Inc. v. Aritzia, Inc.* (2025): **"Style, no matter how creative, is an idea, and is not protectable by copyright."** This tracks decades of consensus. As IP attorney Sy Damle told Congress in 2023: "If I were to write a song in the style of Taylor Swift, that would not be copyright infringement. That's one of the founding precepts of copyright law."

The critical distinction is between *style* and *expression*. Reproducing Jerry Saltz's actual published sentences would be infringement. Generating entirely new text that shares his analytical approach, vocabulary patterns, and rhetorical tendencies is not. The AI outputs new criticism of new artworks — there is no substantial similarity to any specific copyrighted work.

Even if a copyright claim were viable, the fair use defense under § 107 is overwhelming here. The purpose is educational and transformative (new criticism of new artwork). No actual copyrighted text is reproduced. The tool does not compete with or substitute for the critics' published work. All four fair use factors favor the site owner. **Bottom line: copyright risk is negligible.**

## Right of publicity is the real legal battleground

This is where practical risk concentrates. Right of publicity law protects individuals against unauthorized commercial exploitation of their identity — name, likeness, voice, and in some jurisdictions, broader "persona." The question is whether simulating a critic's written analytical voice, using their real name, crosses that line.

**The good news: no court has extended right of publicity to written "voice" or analytical style.** All the landmark voice-appropriation cases — *Midler v. Ford Motor Co.* (9th Cir. 1988), *Waits v. Frito-Lay* (9th Cir. 1992) — involved actual auditory vocal imitation in commercial advertisements. *Waits* explicitly noted that imitation of "singing style" (as opposed to distinctive vocal quality) does not violate right of publicity. Written critical style is even further removed from protectable attributes.

**The bad news: the Carlin precedent is uncomfortable.** In *Main Sequence, Ltd. v. Dudesy, LLC* (C.D. Cal. 2024), George Carlin's estate sued over an AI-generated comedy special that simulated Carlin's voice and comedic style, complete with a disclaimer. The case settled with a permanent injunction against the defendants. While this involved actual AI voice cloning rather than written text, it signals that courts and estates take AI persona simulation seriously regardless of disclaimers.

**State law matters enormously.** Oregon, where the site owner is based, has only common law right of publicity with no statute — among the weakest protections nationally. Oregon protects only name and likeness, not broader "persona" or "style," and recognizes no post-mortem right of publicity. New York, where four of the living critics reside (Saltz, Smith, Krauss, Yau), is more complex. NY Civil Rights Law §§ 50-51 protects "name, portrait, picture, or voice" but is narrowly construed — it does not extend to writing style. However, NY requires the use be "for advertising purposes, or for the purposes of trade," and **§ 50-f explicitly exempts uses with "educational or newsworthy value, including comment, criticism, parody or satire."**

The strongest defenses here are:

- **First Amendment / transformative use**: Under *Comedy III Productions v. Saderup* (Cal. 2001), the key question is whether the work adds "significant transformative elements" or merely exploits celebrity identity. This tool generates original criticism of new artwork using the critics' styles as raw material — it creates something genuinely new. Under the *Rogers v. Grimaldi* test (2d Cir. 1989), uses of identity in expressive works are only actionable if they have "no artistic relevance" to the work or "explicitly mislead" about source. The critics' names are directly relevant to an art criticism tool, and clear disclaimers prevent explicit misleading.
- **Non-commercial/educational nature**: Right of publicity generally targets commercial exploitation. A personal educational website with no revenue falls outside "advertising purposes or purposes of trade." However, this defense weakens immediately if the site has ads, donation buttons, or serves as portfolio promotion.
- **The *Andersen v. Stability AI* dismissal**: In this AI art case (N.D. Cal. 2023), right of publicity claims based on AI creating works "in the style of" named artists were dismissed. Though involving visual art style, the principle extends.

**Practical risk for living critics: low-to-moderate.** The critics would need to establish that written style simulation constitutes appropriation of protectable identity attributes — a legal argument no court has accepted. Combined with First Amendment defenses and the educational context, a lawsuit is unlikely to succeed. But it's not impossible to bring, and defending one is expensive.

## The parody defense does not work here — and that matters

The site owner's instinct that "clearly labeled parody" provides protection is **legally incorrect for this feature.** This is perhaps the most important practical takeaway of the entire analysis.

Under *Campbell v. Acuff-Rose Music* (1994), the Supreme Court defined parody as work that "needs to mimic an original to make its point" through "reference and ridicule" — it must comment *on* the original being parodied. Satire, by contrast, "can stand on its own two feet" and uses borrowed material as a vehicle for broader commentary. **Parody receives significantly stronger legal protection than satire or pastiche.**

"What Would the Critics Say?" does not mock, ridicule, or comment on the named critics. It attempts to faithfully simulate what they would actually say. This is textbook **pastiche or impersonation** — legally distinct from parody and receiving far less protection. The Carlin AI special opened with a disclaimer calling itself "an impression of George Carlin" rather than the real thing. The estate still prevailed.

More critically, the Supreme Court's 2023 decisions tightened the analysis. In *Andy Warhol Foundation v. Goldsmith*, the Court held that adding "new expression, meaning, or message" is **not sufficient by itself** to make a use transformative — the purpose and character of the use matter. In *Jack Daniel's v. VIP Products*, the Court held that parody gets no special First Amendment protection when a mark is used as a source identifier.

**Labeling something "parody" does not make it legally so.** As the Court noted in *Campbell*, parody protection "does not depend on labels" — the content must actually function as parody. The feature should not be marketed or labeled as parody, because that framing invites judicial scrutiny it cannot withstand. Instead, the site owner should lean on the **educational purpose and transformative use defenses**, which are stronger for this specific use case.

## Defamation and false light risks are manageable but not zero

**Defamation risk is low** for several reinforcing reasons. First, all living critics are almost certainly public figures (Saltz won a Pulitzer; Smith was co-chief critic at the *Times*; Krauss co-founded *October*; Yau is a prominent published critic). Public figures must prove **"actual malice"** under *New York Times v. Sullivan* — knowledge of falsity or reckless disregard for truth. A site operator who explicitly labels content as AI-generated simulation is doing the opposite of acting with reckless disregard. Second, defamation requires a "false statement of fact" — AI-generated analysis clearly labeled as simulation is unlikely to be understood by reasonable readers as factual attribution. Third, **defamation claims cannot be brought on behalf of deceased persons**, eliminating risk for Greenberg and Hickey entirely.

The residual risk: if the AI generates criticism that is professionally embarrassing, incompetent, or wildly inconsistent with a critic's known positions, and a reader encounters it without the disclaimer, a living critic could argue reputational harm. The practical defense is disclaimer design.

**False light risk varies by state.** New York does not recognize the false light tort at all — a significant protection for the NY-based critics. Oregon does recognize it (*Dean v. Guard Publishing Co.*, 1985), but the "highly offensive to a reasonable person" threshold is high. Attributing plausible art criticism to a known critic — even if inaccurate — likely does not clear that bar unless the AI generates content that is vulgar, extreme, or deeply contrary to their known positions. False light also requires the defendant to have acted with knowledge of or reckless disregard for the falsity, which the explicit disclaimer undercuts.

**Lanham Act false endorsement (§ 43(a)) risk is moderate but defensible.** Using real critics' names could create an impression of endorsement or affiliation. Courts in the Ninth Circuit (where Oregon sits) have recognized false endorsement claims based on celebrity identity appropriation (*Waits v. Frito-Lay*). However, the Lanham Act requires use "in connection with goods or services" and "in commerce" — a purely non-commercial educational site may fall outside its reach entirely, per *Bosley Medical v. Kremer* (9th Cir. 2005). The *Rogers v. Grimaldi* test provides an additional defense for artistic/expressive works. A recent federal ruling in *Lehrman v. Lovo* (S.D.N.Y. 2025) dismissed Lanham Act claims against an AI voice company, finding that voices used as "tools of their trade, rather than branding assets" lacked the secondary meaning needed for Lanham Act protection.

## Two deceased critics, two different risk profiles — both favorable

**Clement Greenberg (died May 7, 1994, NYC)**: Greenberg presents **essentially zero legal risk**. He died 27 years before New York's post-mortem right of publicity statute (§ 50-f) took effect in 2021, and that statute only applies to individuals who die on or after its effective date. New York has no common law right of publicity. Oregon has no post-mortem right of publicity. Defamation and false light claims die with the person. Greenberg's name and critical persona are effectively in the public domain for these purposes.

**Dave Hickey (died November 12, 2021, Santa Fe, NM)**: Hickey presents **very low risk** but slightly more than Greenberg. He died after NY § 50-f took effect, but he was domiciled in New Mexico — and § 50-f requires New York domicile at death. Under the domicile-at-death choice-of-law rule (*Milton H. Greene Archives v. Marilyn Monroe LLC*), New Mexico law governs his post-mortem rights. **New Mexico has no right of publicity statute and limited common law recognition.** No defamation or false light claims can be brought by his estate. The practical risk is negligible.

## The AI legal landscape is shifting fast — and mostly toward more protection

Several developments from 2023-2026 matter for forward-looking risk:

- **Tennessee's ELVIS Act** (effective July 1, 2024) is the first state law expressly covering AI-generated voice clones, defining "voice" broadly to include simulations. It includes fair use exemptions but signals the legislative trend.
- **The NO FAKES Act** (reintroduced April 2025, pending) would create a federal "digital replication right" covering voice and visual likeness, with post-mortem rights that don't expire. It includes exceptions for commentary, criticism, satire, and parody. Backed by SAG-AFTRA, OpenAI, Disney, and 400+ artists.
- **FTC Revised Endorsement Guides** (June 2023) expanded the definition of "endorser" to cover virtual influencers and AI-generated content. The **FTC Rule on Fake Reviews** (effective October 2024) bans AI-generated fake testimonials with fines up to **$53,088 per violation**. While art criticism isn't a "review" in the consumer sense, this shows regulatory momentum.
- **Oregon's AG guidance** (December 2024) clarified that existing consumer protection law (UTPA, OCPA) applies to AI, including failures to disclose AI involvement.
- **Court rulings on AI training** (*Bartz v. Anthropic*, *Kadrey v. Meta*, both N.D. Cal. 2025) found AI training to be "exceedingly transformative" fair use, but carefully limited holdings to training — not outputs.

The trajectory is clearly toward stronger persona protection in the AI context. A feature that is legally defensible today could face a different landscape in 12-18 months if the NO FAKES Act passes or if the *Authors Guild v. OpenAI* case produces unfavorable precedent.

## Practical recommendations to minimize exposure

The overall risk profile is **moderate without mitigation, low-to-moderate with proper safeguards**. Here are the structural changes and disclaimers that would most reduce legal exposure, ranked by impact:

**Highest-impact changes:**

1. **Redesign the disclaimer architecture.** The disclaimer must appear *before* any AI-generated content is displayed, be at least as prominent as the critics' names, and require user acknowledgment. Suggested language: *"AI SIMULATION — NOT REAL CRITICISM. The following text was generated by artificial intelligence. [Critic Name] did not write, review, approve, or endorse this content. This is an educational tool that simulates a critical approach inspired by published criticism. It does not represent the actual opinions of [Critic Name]."* This disclaimer must repeat with every generated output.

2. **Keep the site strictly non-commercial.** No ads, no donation buttons, no affiliate links, no subscription fees. The moment revenue enters the picture, multiple defenses (NY §§ 50-51 trade purposes exemption, Lanham Act non-commercial use defense, fair use factor one) weaken substantially.

3. **Do not call it "parody."** Frame it as educational/analytical — "an AI educational tool exploring different approaches to art criticism" rather than "a parody of what critics would say." The parody label invites scrutiny the feature cannot withstand, while the educational framing activates stronger protections.

**Medium-impact changes:**

4. **Consider using descriptive labels instead of proper names.** "A formalist critic in the Greenberg tradition" or "A New York School critic" carries far less risk than "Clement Greenberg." If using names is essential to the pedagogical goal, at minimum ensure names appear alongside the disclaimer, never in isolation.

5. **Build in quality controls.** If the AI generates criticism that is incoherent, vulgar, or wildly inconsistent with a critic's known methodology, that increases defamation and false light exposure. Content filtering or a note that "AI simulations may not accurately reflect the named critic's actual analytical approach" adds protection.

6. **Add an educational framing page.** Before users access the tool, present context explaining each critic's actual methodology, the purpose of the exercise, and how AI simulation differs from real criticism. This reinforces the educational purpose that activates First Amendment protections and fair use.

**Lower-impact but still advisable:**

7. **Consider seeking permission from living critics.** A simple email explaining the educational project and requesting consent eliminates right of publicity risk entirely. Some of these critics (particularly Saltz, who is active on social media) might engage positively. Even informal consent documented in writing is valuable.

8. **Monitor the NO FAKES Act.** If federal digital replication legislation passes, reassess the feature immediately. The bill's exceptions for "commentary, criticism, satire, or parody" may protect this use case, but the details matter.

## Conclusion

The "What Would the Critics Say?" feature occupies a legal gray zone — **not clearly illegal, but not clearly safe either.** Copyright provides no viable claim against it. Defamation and false light face high bars. The Lanham Act requires commercial activity the site currently lacks. Right of publicity is the real concern, but existing case law has never extended to written analytical style, and the educational/First Amendment defenses are robust.

The critical insight the site owner should internalize: **this feature is legally defensible not because it's parody (it isn't), but because it's educational, transformative, non-commercial expression generating entirely new content.** The defense strategy should be built on that foundation. With strong disclaimers, strict non-commercial operation, and educational framing, the practical likelihood of a successful legal claim is low. The practical likelihood of receiving a cease-and-desist letter from an annoyed living critic, however, is non-trivial — and the cost of responding to one is the real financial risk for a solo artist/educator in Medford, Oregon.
